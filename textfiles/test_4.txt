A 50-year-old female patient was diagnosed with diffuse large
B-cell lymphoma after presenting with several months of

biopsy confirmed diffuse large B-cell lymphoma. Baseline
PET/CT demonstrated hypermetabolic lymphadenopathy above
and below the diaphragm with splenic involvement.


Bone marrow biopsy showed no evidence of marrow
infiltration. Laboratory studies at diagnosis revealed

ECOG performance status was 1. Based on Ann Arbor staging
criteria, the patient was classified as Stage IIIS
disease. International Prognostic Index assessment placed
the patient in an intermediate-risk category due to stage,
elevated LDH, and extranodal splenic involvement.

Histopathology demonstrated sheets of large atypical
lymphoid cells positive for CD20, CD79a, BCL6, and MUM1.
Ki-67 proliferation index was approximately 80 percent.
The immunophenotype was most consistent with activated
B-cell subtype by Hans algorithm. FISH testing was
negative for MYC, BCL2, and BCL6 rearrangements, with no
evidence of double-hit or triple-hit lymphoma. EBV testing
was negative.

The patient received standard first-line immunochemotherapy
with six cycles of R-CHOP followed by end-of-treatment
PET/CT assessment. Treatment was completed with manageable
toxicities including transient neutropenia, fatigue, and
mild peripheral neuropathy. Interim imaging showed marked
response, and end-of-therapy PET demonstrated complete
metabolic remission.

Approximately eleven months after completion of therapy,
the patient developed recurrent fatigue, enlarging right
axillary lymph nodes, and rising LDH. Repeat PET/CT showed
new hypermetabolic nodal disease in the axillary,
mediastinal, and abdominal regions. Core biopsy of an
axillary node confirmed relapsed diffuse large B-cell
lymphoma with morphology and phenotype consistent with the
original diagnosis.

The patient is therefore assessed as having relapsed
diffuse large B-cell lymphoma after prior first-line
R-CHOP therapy. Current disease status is consistent with
refractory or early relapsing aggressive B-cell lymphoma.
The patient remains a candidate for salvage systemic
therapy, with typical options including R-ICE or R-DHAP

chemosensitive setting, or CAR T-cell therapy depending
on treatment response, eligibility, and multidisciplinary
